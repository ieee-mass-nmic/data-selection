\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

The evaluation asks four questions: does PCU-Select improve selected-data performance, does it amortize cost across PEFTs, which components matter, and how does it behave on unseen configurations?

\subsection{Setup}

We evaluate on four main tasks: GSM8K, HumanEval, MMLU, and TyDiQA. Metrics use each task's native scale: exact match, Pass@1, accuracy, or F1. The candidate pool contains 300K mixed instruction examples, and the main selection budget is 10\%; budget sensitivity also uses 5\% and 30\%. Each method trains the same target backbone, PEFT recipe, number of steps, and evaluation protocol; only the selected subset changes. Reported downstream values average over three target-training seeds.

\input{tables/table_peft_configs}

The seen PEFT set contains attention LoRA, MLP LoRA, (IA)$^3$, and adapters; unseen tests vary rank, placement, learning rate, and family. Baselines include Random and Balanced-Random; heuristic selectors based on length, loss, perplexity, and IFD; representation selectors such as embedding-nearest-neighbor, diversity clustering, and RDS+; and gradient/influence selectors including a LESS-style per-PEFT gradient baseline. Two of these deserve precise, honest definition because their names invoke prior work. \textbf{RDS+} ranks candidates by cosine similarity between per-feature-standardized joint semantic embeddings---the same embedding PCU-Select consumes---and the standardized mean embedding of the task sketch; it is task-conditioned but PEFT-agnostic and carries no tuned hyperparameters beyond the shared sketch. \textbf{LESS-style} scores each candidate by the site-weighted cosine $\sum_\omega \tilde\alpha_p^\omega \cos(g_x^\omega, g_t^\omega)$ between its projected gradient signature and the pooled sketch gradient, and is PEFT-specific through the site weights $\tilde\alpha_p^\omega$; it shares a single projected-gradient datastore and the same 32-example validation sketch across all targets rather than recomputing per-checkpoint Adam-preconditioned gradients, so we keep the ``-style'' qualifier and give the exact deltas from the original LESS in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix-baselines}. PCU-Select uses the default intervention-site scorer, uncertainty penalty, and adaptive cluster quotas unless an ablation changes one component.

\subsection{Main Results}

\input{tables/table_main_results}

Table~\ref{tab:main-results} reports downstream performance at the 10\% budget with per-cell seed dispersion. Averaged across four tasks and five PEFT configurations, PCU-Select scores 42.81, compared with 39.17 for random selection, 41.83 for RDS+, 42.16 for the influence baseline, and 42.73 for LESS. The gains over random (3.64 points) and RDS+ (0.98 points) are large relative to the seed std (at most 0.27 on the PEFT-averaged column) and are the defensible performance claim. Against LESS, the strongest per-PEFT selector, PCU-Select is statistically comparable rather than dominant: the paired difference across the 20 PEFT-task cells is only 0.08 points, with a 95\% bootstrap CI of $[-0.28, 0.43]$ that straddles zero and a Wilcoxon signed-rank $p=0.65$. Consistent with parity, PCU-Select wins 10 of 20 cells and trails LESS on the MLP-only LoRA column by 0.73 points, while LESS trails PCU-Select on the other four columns. Because the four task metrics differ in scale, ceiling, and seed variance, we do not read the averaged points as interchangeable across tasks: the full unaveraged task $\times$ PEFT grid and a per-task normalized comparison are given in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix-per-task} (Tables~\ref{tab:per-task-peft} and~\ref{tab:per-task-normalized}), which show PCU-Select winning on GSM8K and MMLU, tying on HumanEval, and trailing LESS on TyDiQA. We therefore read PCU-Select and per-PEFT LESS as delivering equivalent selection quality; PCU-Select's advantage is that its supervision is computed once and reused across target PEFTs rather than recomputed per configuration, which is what the amortized-cost analysis below quantifies.

Ranking metrics follow the same pattern. At the 10\% budget, PCU-Select reaches mean Spearman $\rho=0.625$, NDCG@K $=0.703$, and pairwise accuracy $=0.793$ against high-fidelity held-out utility labels. LESS reaches $\rho=0.612$, NDCG@K $=0.677$, and pairwise accuracy $=0.765$. The difference is modest but consistent with PCU-Select learning a reusable correction over the site-weighted proxy.

We report no attainable-performance ceiling. We did not run a per-cell oracle (e.g., selecting the subset that maximizes each target PEFT's own downstream metric), so all comparisons here are between practical selectors and none should be read as a gap to an upper bound; we leave a measured ceiling to future work.

\subsection{Amortized Cost}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{Figures/fig_cost_break_even.pdf}
\caption{PCU-Select pays 11.2 GPU-hours offline, then applies cheaply to each target PEFT. It breaks even with per-PEFT LESS/influence recomputation after about 1.6 target configurations. Cheap PEFT-agnostic baselines remain cheaper in raw GPU-hours.}
\label{fig:cost}
\end{figure}

PCU-Select concentrates cost in cacheable offline artifacts: feature extraction, low-fidelity labels, high-fidelity labels, and scorer training. The measured offline cost is 11.2 GPU-hours, dominated by 4.5 GPU-hours of high-fidelity labeling. Online application costs 0.925 GPU-hours per target on average, including selection and fixed target training. Per-PEFT LESS costs 0.996 online GPU-hours plus 6.8 GPU-hours of target-specific gradient recomputation. Figure~\ref{fig:cost} shows that PCU-Select breaks even with LESS after 1.63 target PEFTs. It does not beat RDS+ or random on raw cost; its advantage over those baselines is performance, not cheaper selection.
