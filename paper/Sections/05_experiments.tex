\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

The evaluation asks four questions: does PCU-Select improve selected-data performance, does it amortize cost across PEFTs, which components matter, and how does it behave on unseen configurations?

\subsection{Setup}

We evaluate on four main tasks: GSM8K, HumanEval, MMLU, and TyDiQA. Metrics use each task's native scale: exact match, Pass@1, accuracy, or F1. The candidate pool contains 300K mixed instruction examples, and the main selection budget is 10\%; budget sensitivity also uses 5\% and 30\%. Each method trains the same target backbone, PEFT recipe, number of steps, and evaluation protocol; only the selected subset changes. Reported downstream values average over three target-training seeds.

\input{tables/table_peft_configs}

The seen PEFT set contains attention LoRA, MLP LoRA, (IA)$^3$, and adapters; unseen tests vary rank, placement, learning rate, and family. Baselines include Random and Balanced-Random; heuristic selectors based on length, loss, and perplexity; representation selectors such as embedding-nearest-neighbor, diversity clustering, and RDS+; and two gradient/influence selectors, Influence and per-PEFT LESS. Balanced-Random (cluster-stratified uniform sampling) scores $32.90$ on average, within seed noise of plain Random, so the main table focuses on the strongest representative baselines and the appendix reports the omitted aggregate rows. Three baselines deserve precise definition because their names invoke prior work; the appendix gives full specifications. \textbf{RDS+} ranks candidates by cosine similarity between per-feature-standardized joint semantic embeddings (from a \emph{frozen external} sentence encoder shared by all methods and independent of PCU-Select training) and the task-sketch mean; it is task-conditioned but PEFT-agnostic. \textbf{Influence} scores each candidate by cosine between its projected base-model gradient and the pooled sketch gradient over a \emph{single shared} datastore---the cheap, PEFT-agnostic gradient baseline. \textbf{LESS} is our per-PEFT reimplementation of gradient-influence selection~\cite{xia2024less}: for each target it warms up the adapter, extracts Adam-preconditioned per-checkpoint gradients, random-projects them into a \emph{target-specific} datastore, and ranks by validation-sketch gradient influence, so both its ranking and its cost are recomputed per target. LESS is the strongest and most expensive baseline, and the amortized-cost analysis charges it exactly this per-target recomputation. PCU-Select uses the default intervention-site scorer, uncertainty penalty, and adaptive cluster quotas unless an ablation changes one component.

\subsection{Main Results}

\input{tables/table_main_results}

Table~\ref{tab:main-results} reports downstream performance at the 10\% budget with per-cell seed dispersion. Averaged across four tasks and five PEFT configurations, PCU-Select scores 35.18, compared with 32.29 for random selection, 34.44 for RDS+, 34.68 for the Influence baseline, and 35.14 for LESS. The gains over PEFT-agnostic selectors are the primary quality claim: over the 20 PEFT$\times$task cells, PCU-Select beats RDS+ by 0.74 points (descriptive bootstrap interval $[+0.38,+1.06]$, 17/20 wins) and Influence by 0.50 points (interval $[+0.20,+0.76]$, 18/20 wins). Against LESS, the strongest per-PEFT selector, PCU-Select is a near-tie on aggregate: the paired difference is only 0.04 points, with a descriptive interval of $[-0.26,+0.33]$ that straddles zero and 10/20 cell wins. A task-stratified bootstrap gives a consistent interval of $[-0.15,+0.22]$. We use these intervals as dependence-aware summaries because the cells share the candidate pool, task sketches, scorer training, and model family. By the amortization objective of Eq.~\eqref{eq:quality-tie}, this near-tie with LESS is the design target, not a shortfall---the value claim is settled by cost, next.

The LESS comparison is mixed at the cell level: PCU-Select wins 10 of 20 cells and trails LESS on the MLP-only LoRA column by 0.57 points (per-task summary in Appendix Table~\ref{tab:per-task-compact}). Its advantage over LESS appears only on MMLU; GSM8K and HumanEval fall within seed noise, and TyDiQA shows a CI-excluding deficit that the appendix per-task analysis discusses. Because the four task metrics differ in scale, ceiling, and seed variance, we do not read the averaged points as interchangeable across tasks; the full unaveraged grid and a per-task normalized comparison appear in Appendix Tables~\ref{tab:per-task-peft} and~\ref{tab:per-task-normalized}. The safe reading is therefore narrower than a universal win: PCU-Select improves over PEFT-agnostic selection and near-ties per-PEFT LESS on aggregate, while losing on specific task/configuration slices. Its distinguishing property is that its supervision is computed once and reused across target PEFTs rather than recomputed per configuration, which the reuse-versus-recompute comparison (Section~\ref{sec:reuse}) and amortized-cost analysis below quantify.

Ranking metrics follow the same pattern. At the 10\% budget, PCU-Select reaches mean Spearman $\rho=0.625$, NDCG@K $=0.703$, and pairwise accuracy $=0.793$ against high-fidelity held-out utility labels, ahead of LESS by $0.013$, $0.026$, and $0.028$ on those metrics; Appendix Table~\ref{tab:ranking} gives the full five-metric comparison across all selectors. These ranking labels come from the same short-horizon utility construction that trains PCU-Select, so they support the mechanism; downstream metrics remain the evidence for final task performance. Budget sensitivity is consistent with these results: PCU-Select's edge over both Random and LESS is largest at a 5\% budget and shrinks by 30\%, where all gradient selectors converge (Table~\ref{tab:budget-sensitivity}).

As a full-pool reference, fine-tuning each target PEFT on the entire 300K pool (a 100\% budget) averages 36.75 across the four tasks; PCU-Select reaches 35.18 at the 10\% budget on one tenth of the data, a ratio we treat only as an operational reference because it averages heterogeneous metrics. The full-pool score is not a measured upper bound: at a 30\% budget PCU-Select (36.81) and LESS (36.79) edge slightly above it (Table~\ref{tab:budget-sensitivity}), plausibly by discarding low-utility examples, though we do not claim a measured noise-removal effect.

\subsection{Reuse vs.\ Recompute}
\label{sec:reuse}

\input{tables/table_reuse_quality_cost}

The near-tie in Table~\ref{tab:main-results} raises the question the amortization objective is built to answer: if PCU-Select only matches per-PEFT LESS on quality, why not run LESS \emph{once} and reuse its subset across the registry? Table~\ref{tab:reuse-quality-cost} answers it by placing every selector on a single quality--cost axis over the five seen PEFTs. We add a \textbf{Reuse-one-LESS} condition: run LESS on the cheapest LoRA-default source (L-r8-qv) and apply the identical selected subset to all five targets, so it pays exactly one LESS run (31.6 GPU-hours) and nothing more per configuration.

Reusing one subset does not work. Its aggregate quality is 34.24, a 0.90-point drop from per-PEFT LESS that erases LESS's entire margin over the PEFT-agnostic baselines and lands \emph{below} RDS+ (34.44), a representation selector that costs almost nothing. The per-target breakdown (Appendix Table~\ref{tab:reuse-breakdown}) shows the mechanism: the reused subset equals per-PEFT LESS on its source L-r8-qv by construction, loses little on the neighbouring attention-LoRA target (L-r16-qkvo, $-0.52$), but falls sharply where the target trains different sites (L-r8-mlp, $-1.42$) or a different family (IA3-attnmlp, $-1.08$; AD-b64, $-1.47$). The degradation follows the same structural axes as the motivation study, and it is not specific to this source---no single fixed subset can be simultaneously matched to five subspaces. Paying for gradient influence and then discarding the PEFT condition is therefore strictly worse than either extreme.

PCU-Select occupies the point neither extreme reaches. It preserves LESS-level aggregate quality (35.18 vs.\ 35.14) \emph{and} produces a distinct subset per target, at 72.9 GPU-hours for the five-PEFT registry---$2.2\times$ cheaper than per-PEFT LESS. It is not the cheapest option: Reuse-one-LESS and the PEFT-agnostic selectors spend fewer raw GPU-hours. The claim is quality-preserving amortization, not minimal cost: PCU-Select is the only selector in Table~\ref{tab:reuse-quality-cost} that is at once PEFT-conditioned, per-target customized, and near per-PEFT LESS in quality.

\subsection{Amortized Cost}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{Figures/fig_cost_break_even.pdf}
\caption{PCU-Select pays 72.0 GPU-hours offline, then applies cheaply to each target PEFT. It breaks even with per-target LESS gradient recomputation after about 2.3 target configurations; at the five seen PEFTs in the main evaluation, it spends 72.9 selection GPU-hours versus 158.0 for LESS.}
\label{fig:cost}
\end{figure}

We measure GPU-hours on $8{\times}$NVIDIA A100-80GB (bfloat16) as $(\text{wall seconds}\times\text{GPU count})/3600$. Because every method pays the same shared target-training cost ($6.4$ GPU-hours per configuration---$\approx2.9$\,s/step over $1000$ steps with FlashAttention-2, gradient checkpointing, and length-bucketed batching at an average selected-example length of $\approx512$ tokens, detailed in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix-protocols}), we report the \emph{selection-only} cost that distinguishes the selectors. PCU-Select concentrates its cost in cacheable offline artifacts: feature extraction costs 24.0 GPU-hours, low-fidelity labeling 2.4, high-fidelity short-horizon labeling 42.0, and scorer training 3.6, for 72.0 GPU-hours paid once. The high-fidelity stage averages 1.89 wall-clock seconds per labeled triple, and feature extraction averages 27.8 examples per wall-clock second across the 8-GPU worker, matching the scale of Llama-2-7B forward/backward passes. PCU-Select then spends 0.18 GPU-hours per target for cached-feature reuse, scoring, and clustering. LESS instead recomputes a target-specific Adam-preconditioned gradient datastore for every configuration, at 31.6 selection GPU-hours per target. Break-even is $T=C_{\text{offline}}/(C^{\text{LESS}}_{\text{sel}}-C^{\text{PCU}}_{\text{sel}})=72.0/(31.6-0.18)\approx2.29$ target configurations (Figure~\ref{fig:cost}). OOD targets needing calibration add $2.1$ GPU-hours for $500$ labels---the same high-fidelity labeling routine as the offline stage, at $1.89$\,s per triple---shifting this to at most $\approx2.5$. PCU-Select's cost advantage is amortization; RDS+ and random remain cheaper when raw selection cost dominates.

\paragraph{Takeaway.}
PCU-Select is designed for reuse across a PEFT registry. The five seen PEFTs in the main evaluation already sit past break-even: PCU-Select spends 72.9 selection GPU-hours, while per-PEFT LESS spends 158.0. Unlike the cheaper Reuse-one-LESS shortcut of Section~\ref{sec:reuse}, it reaches this cost without sacrificing per-target quality.
