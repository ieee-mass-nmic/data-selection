\section{Method: PCU-Select}
\label{sec:method}

PCU-Select learns the surrogate in Eq.~\eqref{eq:surrogate} and uses it to select a budgeted subset for a target PEFT and task. Figure~\ref{fig:architecture} shows two stages. Offline, PCU-Select extracts reusable sample and task signals, constructs gradient-proxy and short-update labels, and trains the scorer. Online, it encodes a target $(p^{*},t^{*})$ and measures its structural support from the training registry. It then scores the pool and allocates the budget across semantic clusters. Algorithms~\ref{alg:offline} and~\ref{alg:online} in the appendix specify both stages.

\subsection{Shared Intervention Sites Enable Cross-PEFT Reuse}

PEFT families expose parameter tensors with different shapes and roles, which prevents a shared raw-gradient cache. PCU-Select instead compares them at \emph{intervention sites}: selected module outputs through which PEFT updates affect the backbone. In plain terms, a site records how a sample or task pushes an intermediate activation. This signal remains comparable across PEFT parameter shapes.

PCU-Select hooks attention, MLP, and post-residual outputs at eight evenly spaced Transformer layers, giving $|\Omega|=24$. By the chain rule, PEFT-parameter gradients factor through the upstream gradients at the module outputs they modify. A site gradient therefore provides a shared proxy coordinate without reconstructing any full PEFT-parameter gradient. These coordinates avoid shape mismatches between fused and split projections. The post-residual coordinate also places adapters alongside LoRA and (IA)$^3$.

The site mask records \emph{where} a configuration acts. Separate operator and capacity features record \emph{how} strongly and by what mechanism it acts. This factorization lets all configurations reuse one sample signature without treating additive, multiplicative, and bottleneck updates as interchangeable.

Each PEFT configuration maps to a site mask and an operator type. Attention LoRA and (IA)$^3$-attention touch attention-output sites; MLP LoRA and (IA)$^3$-FFN touch MLP-output sites; adapters touch block-residual sites; and prefix tuning touches attention-output sites. The code represents BitFit as a bias shift. PCU-Select assigns each site a normalized PEFT weight
\begin{equation}
  \begin{aligned}
  w_p^{\omega}
  &=\mathrm{mask}(p,\omega)\,\kappa(p,\omega),\\
  \kappa(p,\omega)
  &=\tanh\!\left(\eta\log(1+\mathrm{cap}(p,\omega)/d_{\mathrm{model}})\right),\\
  \tilde{w}_p^{\omega}
  &=w_p^{\omega}/\max(\varepsilon_w,\sum_{\omega'}w_p^{\omega'}).
  \end{aligned}
  \label{eq:site-weight}
\end{equation}
Untouched sites receive zero weight, while capacity raises the weight of touched sites. Appendix Table~\ref{tab:capacity} maps LoRA rank, adapter width, (IA)$^3$ vector count, prefix length, and BitFit bias count to a trainable-parameter scale. The $\tanh$ limits the influence of high-capacity configurations. PCU-Select encodes operator type separately in $z_p$ because a family-wide scalar would cancel under the normalization in Eq.~\eqref{eq:site-weight}.

\subsection{Shared Representations Preserve Target Specificity}

The three representations answer different questions: what the example contains, what the task needs, and where the PEFT can act. PCU-Select expresses all three in the shared site coordinate system before constructing utility labels.

The sample representation $z_x=[e_x;d_x;a_x]\in\mathbb{R}^{848}$ combines semantic content, difficulty, format, and activation behavior. Its 768-dimensional semantic vector is the frozen \texttt{sentence-transformers/all-mpnet-base-v2} embedding of the instruction and response~\cite{song2020mpnet,reimers2019sentencebert}. We do not train this encoder on the candidate pool or target benchmarks. A 16-dimensional vector records length, response-LM loss, entropy, and format features. A 64-dimensional activation signature summarizes response-token hidden states at the eight sampled layers.

The task representation $z_t\in\mathbb{R}^{848}$ describes what the target task needs. PCU-Select mean-pools $z_x$ over the 32 examples in the task sketch. It also averages their per-site gradients into $g_t^{\omega}$ for the gradient-proxy label. These $24\times256$ gradients remain separate from $z_t$.

The PEFT representation $z_p=[m_p;c_p;r_p]\in\mathbb{R}^{128}$ describes where and how the target configuration acts. A $24\times4$ site/operator mask ($m_p$, 96 dimensions) marks active sites, additive updates, multiplicative scaling, and prefix-style control. Two 16-dimensional vectors encode capacity ($c_p$) and the training recipe ($r_p$). At the 24-site resolution, q/v and q/k/v/o LoRA touch the same attention-output sites. The capacity and recipe vectors preserve their projection-level distinction.

Prefix, P-Tuning, and BitFit use the same deterministic site, operator, capacity, and recipe rules as the seen families. The model uses only these structural features; it has no learned family lookup or operator centroid. Here, \emph{zero-shot} means scoring a new configuration without target-specific short-update labels. When a PEFT family provides such labels, the residual head described below can calibrate the score.

PCU-Select trains from two complementary label sources. Gradient-proxy labels cheaply cover the full candidate pool. Short-update labels measure the effect of actual PEFT updates on fewer examples. Together they provide broad ranking structure and correct it near consequential selection decisions.

The \emph{gradient-proxy label} measures alignment between each sample and the task sketch at sites used by the target PEFT:
\begin{equation}
  u^{\mathrm{lo}}(x,p,t)=\sum_{\omega\in\Omega}\tilde{w}_p^{\omega}
  \cos\!\left(g_x^{\omega},g_t^{\omega}\right).
  \label{eq:ulo}
\end{equation}
Here $g_x^{\omega}$ derives from the response-token loss gradient at site $\omega$. A unit-normalized Johnson--Lindenstrauss random projection~\cite{johnson1984jl} compresses it to a fixed length while approximately preserving its geometry. The sketch mean gives $g_t^{\omega}$. All task sketches use response-token cross-entropy for these signatures. PCU-Select reserves task-native metrics such as option likelihood, Pass@1, and F1 for downstream evaluation. Because the cosine proxy can be negative, PCU-Select replaces raw values with their ranks inside each $(p,t)$ bucket before proxy distillation and ranking. Once it caches sample signatures, this proxy cheaply labels many $(x,p,t)$ triples.

The \emph{short-update label} checks what happens after actual PEFT updates. It corrects the proxy near the selection boundary. For each sampled triple, PCU-Select starts from two checkpoints: the base model and a warm checkpoint after adaptation has begun. It forms a mini-batch $\mathcal{B}(x)$ from the sampled example and seven in-bucket fillers. PCU-Select performs a short PEFT update on this batch, then measures the reduction in sketch loss:
\begin{equation}
  \Delta(x,p,t,a,h)=L_{V_t}(\theta_a)-
  L_{V_t}\!\left(\mathrm{Adapt}^{h}_{p}(\mathcal{B}(x);\theta_a)\right).
  \label{eq:delta}
\end{equation}
Rank-normalized reductions after $h\in\{1,4\}$ update steps from both anchors define $u^{\mathrm{hi}}$. The mini-batch $\mathcal{B}(x)$ estimates a stable mini-batch marginal instead of an isolated single-example gradient. PCU-Select allocates the 10K short-update labels in three stages. Coverage sampling first spreads labels across the registry and needs no scorer.

PCU-Select then trains a provisional scorer on the coverage labels and the gradient proxy. The provisional model supplies rank estimates and an uncertainty scale. Uncertainty sampling targets triples with large predicted uncertainty. Boundary sampling targets disagreements between predicted and observed ranks near selection decisions. Every rank normalization compares labels from the same PEFT, task, anchor, and horizon bucket.

The two label sources serve complementary roles. The gradient proxy cheaply orders the full pool for many PEFT--task pairs. Short-update labels measure realized sketch-loss reduction near the selection boundary. The base and warm checkpoints test whether utility persists after adaptation begins. Two horizons reduce dependence on a single update length. Bucket-wise ranking compares the signals without assuming a shared raw-loss scale across tasks or configurations.

\subsection{Conditional Scoring Produces Target-Specific Subsets}

The scorer combines the three representations and predicts a utility mean and uncertainty scale, $(\hat{\mu},\hat{\sigma})=s_{\phi}(z_x,z_p,z_t)$. Separate encoders first map $z_x$, $z_p$, and $z_t$ into hidden vectors. FiLM modulation~\cite{perez2018film} then uses the PEFT and task codes to rescale and shift the sample features. In concrete terms, the target configuration and task determine which sample features receive more emphasis. A bilinear term directly models compatibility between sample and PEFT features.

Training combines ranking and regression on short-update labels, distillation from the gradient proxy, and a heteroscedastic uncertainty loss. The uncertainty loss allows different triples to have different predicted noise levels. Stage A learns broad conditional structure from the gradient proxy. Stage B enables all losses and selects a checkpoint on held-out PEFT--task pairs. Before downstream evaluation, we fix the sketch size, label allocation, anchors, horizons, and scalar selection hyperparameters on these held-out pairs.

At application time, PCU-Select ranks examples by a conservative score that subtracts a fraction of the predicted uncertainty scale:
\begin{equation}
  q(x)=\hat{\mu}(x,p^{*},t^{*})-\lambda\hat{\sigma}(x,p^{*},t^{*}),\qquad \lambda=0.2.
  \label{eq:qscore}
\end{equation}
The scale acts as a ranking penalty, not a confidence interval. Pure top-$k$ ranking can over-select near-duplicates. PCU-Select therefore clusters examples in semantic space. It allocates budget across $K$ clusters indexed by $k\in\{1,\ldots,K\}$:
\begin{equation}
  b_k=\mathrm{round}\!\left(
  B\frac{(v_k^{+})^{\gamma}|C_k|^{1-\gamma}}
  {\sum_j(v_j^{+})^{\gamma}|C_j|^{1-\gamma}}\right),\qquad \gamma=0.6.
  \label{eq:quota}
\end{equation}
Here $v_k$ is the mean conservative score among the top 10\% of examples in cluster $C_k$, and $v_k^{+}=\max(v_k,0)$. For the 300K pool, $K=\max(50,\lfloor\sqrt{N}\rfloor)$ produces 547 clusters with about 548 examples each. The reported 0.36 GPU-hours per PEFT--task application includes clustering. The final subset takes the top-$b_k$ examples by $q$ within each cluster. Clusters with non-positive value receive no budget. Deterministic remainder handling makes the quotas sum to $B$.

The registry assigns each unseen configuration to one of three support levels. Near-support targets (L0) resemble a seen configuration, far-support targets (L1) make larger shifts within a seen family, and unseen-family targets form L2. Far-support targets use an optional residual calibration head when their PEFT family provides short-update labels. The residual head is a small linear layer that corrects the scorer.

For seen and near-support targets, PCU-Select performs all gradient extraction and label construction offline, and the online path uses only cached features and scorer forward passes. Far-support targets may add a small calibration set, but never rebuild a target-specific gradient datastore. The evaluation next tests whether this reuse preserves LESS-level quality, reduces registry cost, and transfers to unseen configurations.
