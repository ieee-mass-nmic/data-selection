Parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) has become a practical paradigm for adapting large language models under limited computational and storage budgets. Existing data selection methods, however, typically estimate sample utility under a fixed fine-tuning configuration, implicitly assuming that the same subset of training data is equally useful across different PEFT methods.

In this paper, we study a cross-PEFT data-efficient fine-tuning problem: given a target PEFT configuration and a limited annotation or training budget, the goal is to select a compact training subset that generalizes well to the target adaptation method. We first show that sample utilities are only moderately consistent across PEFT methods, suggesting that PEFT-specific adaptation mechanisms can induce different data preferences.

To address this issue, we propose a PEFT-conditioned data selection framework that models sample utility conditioned on both task information and PEFT configuration. The proposed scorer learns from utility signals collected under multiple source PEFT methods and predicts the usefulness of each candidate sample for a target PEFT method. During fine-tuning, we select the top-ranked samples under the target condition and train the target PEFT module only on the selected subset.

Experiments across multiple PEFT configurations, including LoRA, Prefix Tuning, and IA3, show that our method consistently improves over random selection and provides better cross-PEFT transferability under limited data budgets. Further analysis demonstrates the importance of PEFT-aware conditioning, the transfer behavior to unseen PEFT configurations, and the computational advantages of reusing a learned utility scorer.
